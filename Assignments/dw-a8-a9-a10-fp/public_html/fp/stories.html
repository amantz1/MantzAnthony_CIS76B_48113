About the mics...

In this session, all types of microphones were used. Dynamic mics were used on the drums, condensers on percussion, vocals, and sax, and ribbon mics on the brass and guitar. Through expert positioning and the use of dividers, microphone "bleed" was kept to a minimum and a great degree of control was maintained for mixdown.

Putting them all together

In the summer of 2014, I was approached to record a group called The Original Sacrifice Band. The only catch, they are a fairly large band and wanted to do the entire thing live. I was ecstatic because songs today are more "assembled" then played. On September 6th, we walked into East West Studios on Sunset and made it happen. Here is the breakdown of the mics used:

On drums there was an AKG D112 and a reversed NS10 speaker on the kick drum, SM57 on the snare, MD421's on the toms, and U67's as drum overheads. For percussion, there was a pair of C12's on both the timbales and the congas. On the horn section we had R121 ribbons on both the trumpet and trombone, and a vintage C12 on the saxophone. There was also an R121 on the guitar cab. For vocals, we used a U87 on male vocals and 2 U67's on female vocals. Bass and keys ran direct. In all we used a total of 20 mics simultaneously in the same room and managed to record an entire album in only 6 hours. It's too bad most bands don't record this way anymore, because you feel an energy and synegy you just can't get layering samples in a sequencer.
